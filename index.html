The greatest story ever told

Phasellus nec erat sit amet nibh pellentesque congue

Biology teaches us about connections. Connections between your neurons, which can help you read this, connections between organisms in space and time (the latter in which I am specially interested). And ecological connections, in which literally every thing alive will sooner or later give itself to others, in an endless cycle.

But mostly, biology tells us that we, the living things - from those that can read to the ones that don’t even have an eye, from those that have it all to the ones that have but a single cell or hours to live their lives -, have a past in common. Everything (maybe almost everything) alive is a descendent of the same single ancestral, and, since that, we are here, struggling in a world that insists on being hostile to all of us.

But one of life’s signature is that, even in such an unfavorable world, it can always find a way to flourish beauty. This beauty isn’t always crystal clear. Most of the times, we have to search for it. But more than everything, I believe nature’s beauty is inside the questions it whispers in our ears when we are observing its struggle for permanence.

My current work is about searching for good questions of my own about nature’s big patterns, but I’m deeply interested in helping people get to their own questions, by telling them (inside or outside a classroom) the greatest story ever told - the history of life.
